How It Works

The Game

European marble solitaire is a puzzle played on a board with 37 holes and 36 marbles. The center hole starts empty. You move by jumping one marble over another into an empty hole — the jumped marble is removed. The goal is to end with just 1 marble left. It sounds simple, but there are billions of possible move sequences, and most end with you stuck and several marbles remaining.

The AI: A Tiny Brain That Teaches Itself

This AI knows absolutely nothing about marble solitaire when it starts. No rules were programmed in beyond "here's the board and here are the legal moves." Everything else — strategy, patterns, which moves lead to dead ends — it figured out on its own.

The "brain" is a convolutional neural network with about 75,000 parameters (tiny by modern AI standards — ChatGPT has billions). It takes a snapshot of the board and outputs two things:

  • Policy — "Here's how confident I am in each possible move" (a probability for every legal jump)
  • Value — "How good does this board position look?" (a score from -1 for hopeless to +1 for solved)

How It Thinks: Imagining the Future

Before making each move, the AI doesn't just pick the first thing that looks good. It runs a mental simulation called MCTS (Monte Carlo Tree Search) — the same technique used by AlphaGo, the AI that beat the world champion at Go.

Think of it like a chess player thinking ahead: "If I jump here, then I could jump there, then..." The AI imagines hundreds of possible futures from the current position, guided by its neural network. Moves that the network thinks are promising get explored more deeply. After all that "thinking," it picks the move that looked best across all the simulations.

How It Learns: Playing Against Itself

Here's where it gets interesting. The AI trains itself through a loop inspired by AlphaZero (DeepMind's game-playing AI):

  1. Play — The AI plays 50 full games of solitaire using MCTS to choose moves. Early on, its neural network is random, so it plays badly. But it always records what happened: which board positions it saw, which moves it chose, and how the game ended.
  2. Learn — After playing, it trains the neural network on those recorded games. The network learns: "In positions like this, moves like that tend to lead to better outcomes." The policy head learns to suggest better moves. The value head learns to recognize good vs. bad positions.
  3. Repeat — Now the AI plays more games, but this time with a slightly smarter neural network guiding the MCTS. It plays a bit better, generates better training data, which makes the network smarter still.

Each cycle through this loop is called an iteration. Over 250 iterations, the AI transforms from making random jumps to discovering real strategies — all without being taught a single tactic by a human.

A Secret Ingredient: Dirichlet Noise

There's a subtle problem with self-play: if the AI always picks what it thinks is best, it'll keep playing the same games and stop improving. To fix this, we sprinkle random Dirichlet noise into the AI's move probabilities at the start of each MCTS search. This forces it to occasionally try moves it wouldn't normally consider — like a student being told "try a different approach on this problem." Sometimes those random detours reveal strategies the AI never would have found otherwise.

Why doesn't Gen 5 fully solve?

You'll notice Gen 5 consistently gets to 2 marbles remaining but rarely finishes with just one. That's not a bug — it's the honest result of a capped training budget.

Marble solitaire has roughly 109 possible game paths. The reward signal in AlphaZero-style training comes from finishing the puzzle. But if the AI never finishes during self-play, it never sees the "+1.0 you won!" signal — so it has nothing to learn from for that final move. It's like trying to learn how a story ends when you've only ever read the middle chapters.

The full AlphaZero recipe for hard puzzles is: keep training much longer and use much larger networks, until random exploration eventually stumbles into the first complete solution. From there, the model rapidly learns to reproduce it. We chose to stop earlier — so you're seeing what pure self-play discovers within a modest budget. With ~10× the compute, Gen 5 would likely solve. Without seeding it answers, that's where AlphaZero-style training honestly lands.

What You're Seeing

The 5 generations are snapshots across both training phases — you're watching the AI go from random play to near-perfect:

  • Gen 1 — Clueless (Phase 1, iter 1): Random initial weights. It makes legal moves but has no idea what it's doing. Expect 20–30 marbles left.
  • Gen 2 — Beginner (Phase 1, iter 50): A few thousand self-play games in. Picking up local patterns but still flails. ~12–18 marbles.
  • Gen 3 — Intermediate (Phase 1, iter 150): Real strategy emerging. Consistently clears most of the board. ~6–10 marbles.
  • Gen 4 — Advanced (Phase 1, iter 500, end of Phase 1): Strong play across the whole game. ~4–6 marbles.
  • Gen 5 — Master (Phase 2, iter 250, bootstrapped from Gen 4): Near-perfect endgame. Consistently reaches 2 marbles, but the very last move is the wall it hits.

Try them all and compare. The same puzzle, the same neural network architecture, but radically different skill levels — all discovered through self-play, with no human telling the model how to solve.
